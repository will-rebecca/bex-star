What is Obsessive Compulsive Disorder?

A little over 1.2% of US adults had OCD in the past year; way less common than most anxiety disorders.

50.6% of adults with OCD have serious impairment.

Misconceptions about OCD

Although we do not know what causes, it is something that probably starts in childhood and gets a lot worse during teenage and young adult years.

The parts of your brain that are responsible for rewards and error detection movement and memories are not communicating effectively.

Common Misconceptions
  • OCD is common and everyone has it
  • There are no subcategories of OCD (i.e. Focuses on organization, cleanliness, etc.)
  • There is no treatment
  • OCD is neurotic
  • OCD has to do with your control over it

Diagnosis

Definition: OCD is a disorder recognized for its recurrent obsessions and compulsions.

  • Obsessions are unwanted, intrusive, and repeated thoughts, urges, or mental images that cause anxiety.
  • Compulsions are behaviors that a person feels the need to repeat to reduce anxiety to stop obsessive thoughts.
  • The obsessions or compulsions are time-consuming (eg. take more than one hour per day) or cause significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
Symptoms
  • Intrusive and persistent thoughts
  • Compulsions that interfere with a person’s ability to function
  • Symptoms that may start slowly and go away for a while or worsen over time
  • Symptoms that often get worse during times of stress
Cycle of OCD
  • Obsession: Unwanted, recurring intrusive thoughts that cause distress. The content of the obsession varies depending on the type of OCD.
  • Anxiety: obsessions cause fear that result in anxiety (or doubt). You then feel the need to do something to lessen the anxiety, leading to compulsions.
  • Compulsions: may be observable behaviors or mental rituals, such as checking locks, excessive hand-washing, or reassurance seeking.
  • Temporary Relief: Because there is relief after a compulsion, these behaviors become reinforced. Relief is short-lived until the obsession occurs again.

Treatment

CBT or Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, with EXRP or Exposure and Response Prevention is the standard for treating OCD

Many OCD patients also treat their symptoms with medication like SRI’s or Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors.

50% of patients see an improvement with standard treatment, and 70% see progress over time.
